Rock is an umbrella term that defines a musical genre of popular music that developed during and after the 1950s. Punk is a musical and cultural movement, and also used to designate people who follow this movement. Punks initially appeared both in the United States and in Great Britain, in their own local scenes and independent and a few years later they spread to other countries. The punk culture in the USA emerged in the 70's, having as exponent the band Ramones, a rock band with a different dressing style, wearing biker style leather jackets, white t-shirt, jeans, sneakers, showing an aggressive, rebellious appearance, with a musical style, fashion, and a taste for the arts quite different from the traditional ones of that time. The punk movement emerged as a counterpoint to hippie ideology, a reaction to non-violence and its attitudes. Initially it was called punk culture, then it became a movement, and then an ideology, where young people followed the same political side, way of dressing, taste for music, cinema, arts and so on.

Hard rock is a musical style, subgenre of rock that has its roots in garage rock and psychedelic mid-1960s, which is characterized by being considerably heavier than rock music conventional, and marked by the use of distortion, a prominent rhythm section, simple arrangements and a powerful, with heavy guitar riffs and solos. The typical line-up consisted of drums, bass, guitar, and sometimes a piano or keyboard, plus a vocalist who often used vocals sharp and hoarse. In the late 1960s, the terms hard rock and heavy metal were practically used as synonyms, but the latter gradually began to describe a style of music played even louder and intensity.[1] There is yet another key difference between both sub-genres: While hard rock has maintained its blues identity and some swing in the beat, metal melodies are often dictated by riffs guitar aggressive, devoid of swing.
